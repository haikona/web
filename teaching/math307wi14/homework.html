Homework 2, Question 1 (e):

This question is lifted directly from Boyce, corresponding to question 23 of Section 2.3. The solutions at the back of the book assumes that you start with the differential equation $$m \frac{dv}{dt} = -mg - kg\cdot v,$$ where \(m=180\), \(g=32\) and \(k=0.75\) and \(12\) during the freefall and parachute parts of the skydive respectively.

It's not clear to me as to why the second term has that extra \(g\) in it: if I had set up the model, I would have used \(m \frac{dv}{dt} = -mg - kv\), as I'm sure many of you are attempting to do. The latter seems to make more sense to me, but because they never mention what the units of the proportionality constants are it's impossible to ascertain which differential equation is the more correct choice.

Therefore: for this question I'll accept either set of answers as correct, so long as you a) state upfront which of the two differential equations you're working with, and b) remain consistent with your choice thereof throughout the question.

Homework 2, Question 1 (e) iv:

Here you are asked to find the total time taken for the skydiver to reach the ground. In order to do so you'll end up needing to solve for \(t\) in an equation that looks like $$ t = A + Be^{-Ct} $$ for some constants \(A,B\) and \(C\) (that you know). There is no way to solve for \(t\) explicitly here, so you will have to provide an approximate answer.
Hint: note that the \(Be^{-Ct}\) term is very small compared to the constant \(A\) term, so your answer should be very close to \(t=A\).

I've amended the question in the homework printout to reflect the above clarification.

Homework 1, Question 1 (c):

In this question I ask you to integrate the function \(\frac{x+2}{x^2+9}\) by using partial fractions and complex numbers. At some point you'll need to find the antiderivative of \(\frac{1}{x+3i}\).

The correct antiderivative for this function is \(\ln(x+3i)\), without any absolute value signs. The reason why we don't need the absolute value signs here come down to the fact that we're taking the log of a complex number; it turns out that the log of a complex number \(z\) (without absolute value signs) is unambiguously the antiderivative of \(\frac{1}{z}\), so long as \(z\) isn't purely real and negative. This would be the case here - assuming \(x\) is real, \(x+3i\) is definitely not real and negative. It turns out after simplification that the answer you'll get is the same as \(\frac{1}{2}\ln(x^2+9) + \frac{2}{3}\arctan(\frac{x}{3})\), which is what you'd get if you integrated the function in the standard way you learned in MATH125.

Don't worry, I don't expect you to know or remember all this - this is the realm of graduate-level complex analysis, so is way beyond the scope of this course. For the homework I'll accept the answer in the form \((\)complex number\()*\ln(x+3i)\ +\) another term that looks very similar; you need not attempt to simplify beyond this point. The purpose of this question is just to show you that you can employ complex numbers when doing integrals, and to give you a bit of practice doing arithmetic with complex numbers.

Homework Grading Scheme:

There are a total of 7 weekly homework sets for this course. The six best scores will be counted toward your final grade; your lowest homework score will be dropped. I have a strict no late submissions policy for the homework. Requests for extensions will only be considered if submitted in writing (i.e. handwritten note or email) at least two hours before the homework due date and time, and will generally only be given in cases of medical or physical duress.

Each homework set will be graded out of 20 points. Because the course grader is only employed for a maximum of 4 hours a week, given the class size it's unfeasible to have every homework question graded fully. Thus a few questions will be randomly selected from the homework each week and those will be graded fully; this usually comprises about 10-12 points total. The balance of the points will be awarded for fully completing the homework set.

Hopefully this will provide decent feedback on your progress in the course and incentivize you to complete each homework set fully, given the constraints of the grader's contract.
